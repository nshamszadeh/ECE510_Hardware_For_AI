1. I am trying to demonstrate a quantified speedup of the RAVE AI audio synthesis pipeline using a hardware implementation of the audio encoder.
2. Today, RAVE runs entirely in software — either on a CPU or a GPU. CPUs process the audio filterbank and convolutional encoder layers as general-purpose floating point operations, which is flexible but wasteful: these layers have fixed weights after training and a highly regular, repetitive computation pattern that general processors cannot fully exploit. GPU acceleration improves throughput dramatically but draws far more power than the workload requires, making it unsuitable for low-power or embedded deployments. An embedded implementation exists using NVIDIA's Jetson Nano, but this still relies on a general-purpose GPU core rather than dedicated silicon. The fundamental limit is that no implementation targets the specific computational structure of the RAVE encoder in custom hardware — meaning the potential efficiency gains from a purpose-built accelerator remain unrealized.

Profiling RAVE v2 inference (1 second of 48 kHz audio, Intel Core Ultra 7 155H, 10 runs) measured the encoder stage at 25.67 ms per run, yielding a throughput of approximately 135 GFLOPS — only 9% of the CPU's theoretical 1,488 GFLOPS compute ceiling. The root cause is that the encoder's 61.5 MB weight set overflows the 24 MB L3 cache, forcing all weights to be re-streamed from DRAM on every inference frame. The analytically derived arithmetic intensity of the encoder is 53.5 FLOP/byte, which nominally places it in the compute-bound region of the CPU roofline (above the ridge point of 12.45 FLOP/byte), but the L3 overflow means the compute units spend most of their time stalled waiting for data — the effective bottleneck is memory, not arithmetic throughput.
3. What's new in my approach is a custom hardware accelerator that eliminates the specific bottleneck identified by profiling: the repeated DRAM streaming of the encoder's 61.5 MB weight set. By storing all weights in on-chip SRAM and loading them once at startup, the per-frame off-chip data traffic drops to just 216 KB (activations only), raising the effective arithmetic intensity from 53.5 to 15,922 FLOP/byte. This moves the design point far into the compute-bound regime on the roofline — the hypothetical accelerator (4,096 GFLOPS peak, 2,048 GB/s on-chip SRAM bandwidth, ridge point at 2.0 FLOP/byte) sits above the CPU compute ceiling, representing a projected ~30× improvement over the measured 135 GFLOPS software baseline. This approach will work because the encoder's computation is structurally ideal for custom silicon: fixed post-training weights, a regular Conv1d dataflow that maps directly onto a systolic MAC array, and a compact latent output (12 KB) that keeps the host interface bandwidth requirement negligible.
